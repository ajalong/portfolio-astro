_Context_
## Exploring how a new brand could live online

In 2023, SNC-Lavalin contracted Interbrand for a ground-up rebrand. The result was AtkinsRéalis, a unified identity for the Canadian-headquartered global engineering and project management firm formed through the integration of SNC-Lavalin, Atkins and Faithful+Gould. With around 37,000 employees working across infrastructure, nuclear, transportation, energy and defence in over 160 countries, AtkinsRéalis is a sprawling business, and the rebrand was an attempt to bring its many parts under a single, coherent identity.

While the wider Interbrand team developed the new brand, Jonny Buck (UX Researcher), Lisa Pink (Project Manager) and I were tasked with exploring how it could be expressed online. The deliverable was a research-led report with low-fidelity mockups illustrating tested directions, rather than a launched site. The site itself would follow once strategy, brand and our findings had all landed.

[For context on the 2023 rebrand, see Creative Pool's coverage.](https://creativepool.com/interbrandlondon/projects/atkinsrealis-for-atkinsrealis)

_Problem_
## Translating strategic ambitions into a workable digital structure

The brand objectives and the IA challenge were entwined. AtkinsRéalis wanted to position itself as a globally inclusive employer and partner, attracting talent and clients across markets and disciplines. Those ambitions had to live somewhere on the site, and the most consequential place they would land was in how the company organised its sprawling offering. Three problems sat at the centre of the brief.

- **Communicating inclusivity and accessibility credibly.** The brand wanted to make clear that AtkinsRéalis was a place where people of all backgrounds were welcome, both as employees and as clients. Saying so on a values page is easy. Designing a site that demonstrated it through every interaction was the harder problem.
- **Conveying a genuinely global business.** The site needed to feel international by default rather than as a region-switcher tacked onto a UK or Canadian default, and to give visitors from any market a clear path to the work relevant to them.
- **Information architecture for a sprawling offering.** Engineering services, nuclear, transportation, energy, defence, water, infrastructure, project management, across markets, disciplines and legacy brands. The IA had to make sense of this breadth in a way that helped users find what they needed without flattening the company's actual range.

_Approach_
## Designing in parallel with the rebrand

The project ran alongside three other Interbrand workstreams: strategy, brand identity and CX. Our work had to inform all of them and be informed by them in turn, while none of the four was ever fully finished at the same time. That parallelism was the defining condition of the project.

- **Building on strategy as it evolved.** The rebrand strategy was the upstream input for everything we did, and at the time we were exploring digital expression, it was still being developed. We structured the UX work as a series of design sprints, each focused not on testing a brand tenet directly, but on how that tenet could be expressed through design. Show, don't tell. The output of each sprint was tested while the next one ran, which kept the work moving even as strategy decisions further up the chain continued to evolve.
- **Working with the brand workstream as their identity took shape.** The brand identity was being designed in parallel by Adam Johnson's team. We tested how brand tenets translated into digital interactions, and provided broader accessibility guidance to the brand team, helping them understand the constraints digital use would place on the identity they were developing.
- **Establishing what product design was inside Interbrand.** The digital solutions team had previously been a separate agency within Omnicom, Interbrand's parent group, and had only recently merged into Interbrand. This was the first project in which the team operated as part of Interbrand directly. A meaningful part of the work was helping colleagues, and the client, understand what product design contributed, why it mattered, and how it differed from brand-led design.

_Design_
## Show, don't tell

The design work was structured as a series of weekly sprints, each focused on a different surface of the site. The output of each sprint was tested with users while the next ran. The principle running through all of it was show, don't tell. Wherever the brand wanted to communicate something, whether internationality, inclusivity, or expertise, we tested whether it landed better as a statement or as something demonstrated through the interaction itself.

_Case studies_
### A hybrid of map and grid

Showcasing project work was central to the homepage and we tested two approaches. A world map view placed projects geographically, with the international nature of the business visible at a glance. A grid view presented projects as a filterable, sortable collection. The map achieved the brand goal cleanly, but users struggled to use it for finding work. They often did not know where unfamiliar places sat on a world map. The grid won decisively for usability and, unexpectedly, for communicating the variety of the business too.

> "[The grid view] was the one that showcased the variety of work best, as I could see before filtering at the bottom of the page the several different projects running. Also on the filtering, I could see all these sections and subsections that they have projects on."
>
> — Testing participant

We proposed a hybrid that used each view for what it did best. A world map on the homepage with case studies animating in and out from across the globe expressed the international nature of the business as visual atmosphere. The moment a user engaged with the search bar or filters, the view switched to the grid for finding and comparing actual work. The map carried the brand message; the grid carried the task.

_Navigation_
### Depth, not minimalism

Four navigation variants were tested, ranging from a simple dropdown to mega navs of varying complexity. The clear winner was the most descriptive option: a conventional mega nav with full taxonomy groupings exposed at the top level. This cut against the more minimal directions we had also tested. AtkinsRéalis users were engineers, project managers and procurement leads who came to the site looking for specific capabilities, and they wanted depth and clarity rather than progressive disclosure.

> "It had the most descripted details with no other distractions making it in my opinion the most capable one of helping you find your destination more easily."
>
> — Testing participant

_Homepage_
### A hybrid, again

Two homepage variants took different approaches to what sat above the fold. One led with search and a purpose statement; the other led with content tiles surfacing case studies, articles and projects directly. Neither won outright. Participants found the search-led variant welcoming and immediately impressive, but the content-led variant gave the impression of an established firm that knew its work. The recommendation was a hybrid that combined the search-led opening with content surfaced immediately below.

A poll embedded in the homepage was tested separately and unanimously well-received. Participants responded to the implicit signal that the company wanted their input.

> "Having the poll makes it feel like my voice matters, like I can make a difference when I work with this company, it makes me feel part of it."
>
> — Testing participant

This was the inclusivity finding the brand strategy had been reaching for. Not a statement about welcoming people, but an interaction that made them feel addressed. Show, don't tell.

_Project pages_
### People, not abstractions

Within individual case study pages, we tested variants that emphasised different aspects of the work: the technical achievement, the strategic outcome, the people and partners involved. Variants foregrounding partners and team members performed strongest. Participants consistently described these as more legitimate, trustworthy and human.

> "I like how they have included the partners and the team, it makes it more legitimate and real."
>
> — Testing participant

For a global engineering firm, this was a meaningful finding. Trust in this sector is built on people and relationships, not just capabilities. The project pages needed to lead with both.

_Typography_
### A custom typeface, tested

We also helped the brand team test the readability of the custom typeface NaN had developed for the identity. Participants rated it highly across the board for readability at body and display sizes, with no usability issues surfacing during other tests.

_Impact_
## Recognised value, deferred implementation

The project was always scoped as exploration, with a research-led report and tested directions as the deliverable. By that measure, the work delivered. We handed over a substantive body of validated thinking on IA, navigation, case studies, homepage structure, inclusivity messaging and accessibility, all grounded in user testing rather than assertion.

The .com itself received a reskin for the brand launch rather than a ground-up overhaul. Deadlines and delays further up the chain, particularly around strategy sign-off, left no time to implement our recommendations in full ahead of go-live. AtkinsRéalis indicated their intention to incorporate the findings into a future overhaul of the site.

What the project did achieve was a clear shift in how the client thought about the digital experience. By the end of the engagement they understood the value of treating the site as a structural problem rather than a styling exercise, and recognised the specific contribution our work had made: a defensible IA direction, evidence-backed patterns for case studies and homepage structure, and a concrete way of demonstrating inclusivity through interaction rather than statement. For Interbrand internally, the project also marked the first major delivery with the digital solutions team operating as part of the agency directly.

_Lessons learned_
## What the project taught me about exploratory work

The project's structure and outcome surfaced lessons that have shaped how I approach exploratory work since. Most are about what makes research-led design hold up against the realities of complex client engagements.

- **Show, don't tell, is a structural choice as much as a creative one.** The most useful findings on this project were not about how to communicate the brand's values, but about which interactions made those values feel true. Inclusivity landed when users felt addressed by the site, not when the site told them they were welcome. Internationality landed when the case studies animated in from across the globe, not when a banner declared a global presence. For brand-driven projects, this is the discipline. Turning ambitions into interactions, not statements.
- **Test organising principles, not finished structures.** When the brief involves real complexity, such as a multi-sector global firm with several upstream workstreams still moving, the senior move is to test directions in parallel rather than commit early to a single structure. It produces more defensible recommendations and protects the work against the inevitable late-stage strategy shifts that would otherwise force a redesign.
- **Designing in parallel with strategy needs a deliberate working method.** Working alongside strategy and brand workstreams that are themselves still being defined is genuinely difficult. The way through is to design at fidelities that honestly reflect how settled the inputs are, and to structure the work as iterative sprints rather than a linear path to a final deliverable. Both decisions kept this project moving when waiting would have stalled it.
- **An exploration project's value is in what it teaches the client.** Not every engagement produces a launched site, and some never were going to. The success criterion for an exploration project is whether the client ends it understanding their own problem more clearly than they did at the start. By that measure, this project closed well. AtkinsRéalis ended the engagement with a more defensible view of how their digital experience needed to work, even if the timeline did not allow for full implementation immediately.
